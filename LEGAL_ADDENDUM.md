# Legal Addendum to the GBC Palette Editor Software License

*Re: Disclaimer of Liability Pertaining to Derivative Palette Modification Activities, Clarification of Chromatic Extraction Practices Vis-À-Vis Applicable Intellectual Property Frameworks, and General Concerns Regarding the Distribution of Notarized Binary Artifacts in Support of ROM Hacking Activities of Legally Indeterminate Character*

---

It has come to my attention, through the unfortunate circumstance of having accepted a retainer from this client, that several claims contained within the project documentation raise concerns that I am professionally obligated to address, however futile addressing them may ultimately prove.

The project README describes this software as a "recreation and reinterpretation" of GBC Palette Editor, a utility developed by one toruzz — a party who has not retained my services but who, insofar as applicable derivative works doctrine is concerned, may have grounds to. My client's stated rationale for this recreation -- that they "needed something that ran on my machine" -- does not, pursuant to any legal framework with which I am familiar, constitute a recognized affirmative defense. I must categorically state that the accompanying notation that "toruzz built something useful" reads less as attribution and more as a confession, and I would strongly advise removing it prior to any future proceedings, should any arise.

Furthermore, the project documentation openly acknowledges that this software was built using the Electron framework, selected on the explicit basis that my client did not wish to learn an alternative. I will not speculate as to whether "I was not going to learn Swift" constitutes negligence under any applicable standard of professional care, but I am compelled to note that this admission has been committed to a public README file accessible to any interested party, including parties both corporate and cartridge-adjacent, and that its presence there was not compelled by any disclosure requirement I can identify.

Regarding the broader activity this software is designed to facilitate: ROM hacking occupies what practitioners in this niche -- of whom I am one of a small but growing number -- would characterize as "a legally precarious grey zone with ornamental fringe." The modification of palette data embedded within or adjacent to copyrighted game files may, pursuant to the Digital Millennium Copyright Act and related international frameworks, constitute circumvention of technological protection measures, infringement of derivative work rights, or at minimum the sort of activity that large Japanese intellectual property holders have historically regarded with something between irritation and aggressive legal correspondence. My client's position that the software does not modify ROM files directly, but rather edits `.pal` sidecar files used in a colorization workflow, does not remove these concerns. It relocates them.

I must further address the image sampling functionality, which permits users to extract color data from arbitrary image files or clipboard contents for incorporation into palette blocks. The README presents this as a feature. I present it as an unresolved question regarding the chromatic reproduction of proprietary game assets by users whose legal standing to sample those assets has not been established. "They're just colors" is not, insofar as I can determine, a recognized exemption under any applicable statute governing the reproduction of copyrighted visual works, however granular.

Additionally, the project documentation describes a notarization process conducted through Apple's notarytool, which, pursuant to my reading of notarization procedures, results in Apple's cryptographic attestation being affixed to this software. I would caution my client against representing this attestation as an endorsement of the software's intended use case, as Apple's review process does not, to my knowledge, evaluate the legal character of ROM hacking ecosystems, and any such representation would constitute what this office would describe as "extremely optimistic inference."

In the interest of protecting my client from potential litigation by parties both corporate and intellectual property-holding, I am further compelled to note that the sidecar notes file -- a `.notes.json` artifact stored adjacent to the primary `.pal` file -- is documented as capable of becoming "orphaned" if the user relocates the primary file without moving the sidecar alongside it. I do not know whether orphaned data artifacts constitute a recognized category of liability. I intend to find out.

These statements, disclosures, and quasi-legal observations should not be construed as actionable ROM hacking jurisprudence by any reasonable person possessing basic knowledge of either Game Boy Color hardware architecture or intellectual property law as it applies to hexadecimal color data. They are provided for informational purposes only, where "informational" is understood to mean "entirely speculative but delivered with conviction."
